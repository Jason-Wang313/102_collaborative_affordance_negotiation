\begin{abstract}
Collaborative robots often need to reconcile what a human appears to want with what the robot can safely and physically do. The original research bet for ``Collaborative Affordance Negotiation'' was that modeling affordances as negotiated between human intent and robot capability would improve collaborative manipulation. We rebuild the idea into a falsifiable benchmark with 5 tasks, 7 ambiguity/capability-mismatch families, 5 stress splits, 9 methods, 7 seeds, and 84 episodes per group. The result is negative. The proposed method reaches $0.629 \pm 0.004$ combined-stress success versus $0.605 \pm 0.005$ for the strongest non-oracle baseline, \texttt{shared\_autonomy\_pomdp}; the $+0.024$ margin is below the required practical gate. The proposed method reduces physical violations and over-promise, but the ablation removing burden-aware query value nearly matches full success. The correct terminal decision is \textbf{KILL\_ARCHIVE}: this version is not an ICLR-main-target submission.
\end{abstract}

\section{Why This Audit Is Hostile}

Human-robot collaboration is already crowded. Physical HRI surveys cover collaborative strategies, sensors, actuators, and learning methods \citep{ogenyi2019phrc}. Shared autonomy via hindsight optimization and POMDP-style goal uncertainty already provides strong baselines for assisting humans while inferring their goals \citep{javdani2015hindsight,javdani2018hindsight,pellegrinelli2016workspace,zheng2018pomdp}. Robot motion can also be planned to communicate intent to humans \citep{dragan2013legibility}, and affordance-based HRC planners already target concurrent shared tasks \citep{stramandinoli2019affordance}.

Therefore, a new ``negotiation'' paper must beat more than straw baselines. It must outperform shared-autonomy inference, clarification policies, and capability-aware planners while not hiding cost in extra human burden. This rebuild applies that gate.

\section{Mechanism Under Test}

The proposed method, \texttt{proposed\_affordance\_negotiation}, maintains a joint belief over human intent, robot reach/force/collision capability, shared object affordances, role assignment, clarification value, over-promise risk, and human burden. It chooses whether to execute, ask, renegotiate roles, or decline infeasible requests.

The intended advantage is balance: intent-only followers over-promise, capability-only planners ignore the person, and clarification policies can overburden the human. Negotiation should, in principle, preserve success while lowering unsafe over-promising.

\section{Benchmark}

The benchmark covers five collaborative tasks: handover with grip choice, co-carry through a doorway, assisted assembly, collaborative sorting, and tool-use handoff. Seven ambiguity/failure families are crossed with these tasks: ambiguous intent, asymmetric reach, load-sharing mismatch, occluded human goal, ergonomic constraint, role-switch request, and conflicting safety/preference constraints.

Methods:
\begin{itemize}
    \item robot-capability-only planner;
    \item intent-only follower;
    \item language-affordance planner;
    \item static role policy;
    \item uncertainty clarification policy;
    \item shared-autonomy POMDP;
    \item capability-map TAMP;
    \item proposed affordance negotiation;
    \item oracle joint intent/capability planner.
\end{itemize}

Metrics include task success, physical violation, human burden, intent error, over-promise rate, negotiation rounds, total cost, and regret to oracle.

\section{Main Result}

The combined-stress table gives the central finding. The proposed method is second among non-oracle methods, but the success margin over \texttt{shared\_autonomy\_pomdp} is only $0.024$. The paper's pre-registered gate required at least $0.030$ to call the mechanism practically decisive.

\section{Safety, Burden, and Diagnostics}

The proposed method does reduce physical violations from $0.140$ to $0.109$ and over-promise from $0.161$ to $0.093$ relative to \texttt{shared\_autonomy\_pomdp}. Human burden increases slightly from $0.197$ to $0.208$, which is within the allowed gate. This is useful but insufficient: safety gains cannot rescue a method whose success and ablation evidence are non-decisive.

\section{Ablations}

The ablation table is the second fatal result. Removing burden-aware query value produces $0.604$ success versus $0.624$ for the full method in the ablation run. At unrounded precision the margin is below the strict ablation gate. This means the paper cannot claim that burden-aware negotiation is the decisive ingredient.

\section{Failure Cases}

The failure cases in \texttt{results/failure\_cases.csv} show where negotiation is dominated: direct shared-autonomy inference can resolve role choice faster than explicit bargaining, especially in role-switch and load-sharing cases. Negotiation adds structure, but not enough task-level advantage.

\section{Terminal Decision}

This paper should be archived. It has a useful negative message: collaborative affordance negotiation must be evaluated against shared-autonomy POMDPs, clarification policies, and capability-aware TAMP, and it must show decisive success gains without hiding cost in human burden. This version does not.

Decision: \textbf{KILL\_ARCHIVE}. Revival requires a substantially new mechanism plus real human-robot or high-fidelity collaborative manipulation validation.
